## Executive summary

Clinicians read trial results every week and act on them, but the **statistics that decide how a
result should change practice are routinely misread** — by trainees and senior physicians alike.
The literature documents persistent, specific errors: interpreting a **hazard ratio** as if it
were a simple risk ratio or a constant "X% lower risk over the whole study"; reading a
**Kaplan–Meier** curve's tail without regard to how few patients remain at risk; treating a
non-significant **p-value** as proof of no effect, or a significant one as proof of importance;
conflating **relative** risk reduction with **absolute** benefit (and ignoring number-needed-to-
treat); trusting **surrogate endpoints** (PFS, ORR, pCR) as if they were overall survival; and
being fooled by **immortal-time bias**, **lead-time bias**, **competing risks**, post-hoc
**subgroup** claims, and **non-inferiority** framing. These are not esoteric: they change whether a
drug looks worth its toxicity and cost.

This project builds an **open, versioned library of plain-language explainers** — each one teaching
a single statistical concept the way a good colleague would: a crisp plain definition, the precise
technical definition, a **reproducible worked example built on open/synthetic data**, an explicit
"**common trap / what people get wrong**" section, a "**how to read this in a paper**" checklist,
full citations, and a standing "educational, not clinical advice" frame. Alongside the prose, a
small **MIT-licensed, reproducible figure/interactive toolkit** generates every chart from a
committed script on openly-licensed or synthetic data (so a reader can see *why* a hazard ratio
under non-proportional hazards is misleading, not just be told).

Risk tier is **medium**. The dominant risk is **not** infrastructural and **not** privacy (we host
no patient data); it is **getting the statistics wrong** — publishing an authoritative-sounding
explanation that is itself subtly incorrect, which would mis-train the very clinicians we mean to
help. The plan therefore front-loads three hard gates: a **statistical-accuracy gate** (credentialed
biostatistician/epidemiologist sign-off, non-skippable, with a zero-tolerance rule on stating a
statistical falsehood), a **reproducibility gate** (every number in every example is regenerated by
a committed script on a cited open dataset in CI — no hand-typed figures), and a **licensing &
provenance gate** (every example dataset's license verified; every conceptual claim cited to an
authoritative methods source). A standing escalation rule sends any patient-facing rendering or any
treatment-recommendation drift to `riskTier: high` (oncologist **and** patient-advocate sign-off +
"not medical advice"), or excludes it.

## Problem & beneficiaries

**Who is helped.**
- **Practicing clinicians & oncologists** — who must decide whether a published result should
  change what they do, and need to read survival statistics correctly to do so.
- **Trainees: residents, fellows, medical/nursing/pharmacy students** — for whom critical
  appraisal of the oncology literature is a core competency that is unevenly taught.
- **Journal clubs & tumor boards** — which need shared, correct, citable explainers and checklists
  to ground recurring "what does this HR actually mean?" discussions.
- **Patient advocates and research-literate patients** — who increasingly read trial results and
  need an honest, plain explanation of what a curve or a hazard ratio does and does not say (as a
  *comprehension* aid — **not** medical advice; any patient-*facing* rendering is a separate
  `high`-risk deed, see Scope).
- **Medical educators & OER repositories** — who need openly-licensed, reusable, expert-reviewed
  teaching modules they can drop into a curriculum.

**The verified need.** The *general* need is well established and documented: studies of physicians
and trainees repeatedly find systematic misinterpretation of hazard ratios, confidence intervals,
p-values, and surrogate endpoints, and the methodological-literacy gap in reading oncology trials
is a recognized, long-standing problem. We treat that general need as real. The **per-partner,
per-output need is TO BE SECURED**: we have **not** yet confirmed a named training program,
education committee, journal-club network, OER repository, or advocacy organization that has agreed
to *adopt and use* these explainers. Until a named beneficiary confirms adoption, every task carries
`verifiedNeed: false`. This is load-bearing under the Elyos bar ("delivered, not merged"): a polished
explainer library that no one teaches from is **not** a delivered good deed.

**Partner / requestor.** TO BE SECURED. Candidate channels (none assumed, all require outreach):
oncology training programs and their education/critical-appraisal faculty; education committees of
professional societies; open journal-club / evidence-based-medicine communities (e.g.
Students 4 Best Evidence-style networks); OER medical-education repositories (e.g. MedEdPORTAL-style
submission paths); patient-advocacy organizations that train advocates to read the science; and the
EQUATOR/reporting-guideline community. M0 includes explicit beneficiary-outreach work.

## Goals and non-goals

**Goals**
- Produce an **open, versioned library of plain-language statistics explainers for clinicians**,
  each expert-reviewed, fully cited, and built to a shared authoring template.
- Make every explainer **correct first**: a credentialed biostatistician signs off, and a
  zero-tolerance rule forbids shipping a statistical falsehood (including a wrong "common trap").
- Make every worked example **reproducible**: each number and figure is regenerated by a committed
  script on a cited **open/synthetic** dataset, verified in CI — no hand-typed statistics.
- Make every **conceptual claim provenanced**: each definition, interpretation, and pitfall cites
  an authoritative methods source (peer-reviewed biostatistics literature, recognized textbooks,
  reporting guidelines such as CONSORT/SAMPL/STROBE/ICH E9).
- Teach the **misinterpretations explicitly** ("the trap"), because the value is correcting how
  clinicians already (mis)read results, not just restating definitions.
- Provide reusable **"how to read this in a paper" checklists** clinicians can apply at the bench
  of a journal club or tumor board.
- Ship a small **reproducible figure/interactive toolkit** (MIT) so concepts are shown, not just
  asserted, and so figures are auditable and regenerable.
- Be **accessible and translation-ready**: alt-text on every figure, readability targets,
  colorblind-safe curves, and text/figure separation that a later i18n effort can localize.

**Non-goals**
- We do **not** give medical advice, treatment recommendations, prognoses, dosing, or clinical
  decision support, and we do **not** tell clinicians which drug/regimen to choose.
- We do **not** produce **patient-facing** content in this project (a separate `high`-risk deed:
  oncologist + patient-advocate sign-off + "not medical advice" framing required).
- We do **not** rank trials, adjudicate "which study is right", or issue evidence grades / clinical
  recommendations; we teach *how to read* the statistics, not *what to conclude clinically*.
- We do **not** use individual patient data, controlled-access data (dbGaP/EGA/individual-level
  biobanks), or any identifiable records; examples are open-aggregate, openly-licensed teaching
  datasets, or synthetic.
- We do **not** reconstruct individual-patient data from published curves in any way that could
  re-identify a person; any curve-derived pseudo-data is synthetic-by-construction, drawn only from
  openly-licensed published figures, and labelled as such.
- We do **not** invent statistics, "reasonable" effect sizes, or numbers a source does not state or
  a script does not produce.
- We do **not** assert that any explainer constitutes biostatistical *consulting* for a specific
  study; it is general methods education.

## Scope

**In scope**
- A **curriculum map** of clinician-facing statistical concepts (survival analysis core →
  trial-reading → advanced/bias topics), prioritized by how often each is misread.
- A shared **explainer authoring template** (learning objectives · plain definition · precise
  definition · reproducible worked example · "common trap" · "how to read it in a paper" checklist ·
  citations/provenance · "not clinical advice" frame · reviewer sign-off block).
- **Plain-language explainers** for: censoring & Kaplan–Meier curves; median survival & "at-risk"
  tables; **hazard ratios** & the proportional-hazards assumption; log-rank test; Cox regression
  (what it adjusts for and assumes); **confidence intervals vs p-values**; absolute vs relative risk
  & **NNT/NNH**; **surrogate endpoints** (PFS/DFS/ORR/pCR) vs overall survival; **restricted mean
  survival time (RMST)**; **competing risks**; **immortal-time** and **lead-time** bias; **subgroup
  analysis & multiplicity**; **non-inferiority/equivalence** trials; **forest plots** &
  heterogeneity; missing data/attrition basics.
- A **reproducible figure/interactive toolkit** (MIT) generating every figure from a committed
  script on open/synthetic data; optional interactive widgets (e.g. KM-curve generator, "HR under
  non-proportional hazards" simulator).
- A **claims/provenance ledger** linking each statistical assertion to an authoritative source, and
  a **worked-example data manifest** recording each dataset, its license, and de-identification
  status.
- Accessibility (alt-text, readability targets, colorblind-safe palettes) and **translation-ready**
  text/figure separation.

**Out of scope**
- **Patient-facing content of any kind**, clinical decision support, prognostic tools, treatment/
  dosing guidance, or "which drug is better" conclusions (separate `high`-risk deed; oncologist +
  advocate gated).
- Biostatistical **consulting** on, or re-analysis verdicts about, any specific real trial.
- Individual patient data, controlled-access data (dbGaP/EGA/individual-level biobanks), and any
  identifiable records — including individual-level rows in supplements.
- Evidence grading / clinical recommendations / "this study is right" adjudication.
- Software that performs clinical calculation on a *patient's own* data (that would be a regulated
  clinical tool, not an explainer).
- Re-hosting copyrighted textbook figures or paywalled paper content; we cite, we don't copy.

## Solution approach & architecture

This is a **content + light-tooling project**: openly-licensed prose explainers plus a small,
well-tested, MIT figure/interactive toolkit. It hosts no patient data and runs no clinical service.

**Authoring pipeline (per explainer):**
1. **Concept selection & scoping.** Pull the next concept from the curriculum map; define the
   single misconception it must correct and the target reader (clinician/trainee).
2. **Source gathering & provenance.** Collect authoritative methods sources for every claim
   (peer-reviewed biostatistics, recognized textbooks, reporting guidelines). Nothing is asserted
   without a citable source recorded in the **claims ledger**.
3. **Worked-example design & data gate.** Choose an **open/synthetic** dataset for the example;
   record it in the **data manifest** with license + de-identification status; the **licensing &
   data gate** decides PASS/EXCLUDE. Individual patient data is never used.
4. **Reproducible computation.** Write a committed script that produces every number and figure in
   the explainer from that dataset. No statistic appears in prose unless the script emits it.
5. **Drafting to template.** Author the explainer to the shared template, including the explicit
   "common trap" and the "how to read it in a paper" checklist.
6. **Statistical-accuracy review (blocking).** A credentialed biostatistician/epidemiologist
   verifies every definition, interpretation, trap, and computed figure. Zero-tolerance on a stated
   falsehood. This gate cannot be skipped.
7. **Clinical-framing & plain-language review.** An oncologist/clinician checks that framing is
   correct and non-misleading and stays *methods education* (no treatment advice); a plain-language/
   accessibility pass checks readability, alt-text, and colorblind-safe figures.
8. **Release & adoption.** A human publishes the versioned explainer (CC-BY) and the steward records
   any adoption ("delivered") signal. No automated claims of adoption.

**Authoring template (source of truth; site/PDF are projections).** Each explainer carries:
- `id`, `title`, `concept`, `targetReader`
- `learningObjectives[]`
- `plainDefinition` (colleague-to-colleague), `preciseDefinition` (technically exact)
- `workedExample { datasetRef, scriptRef, figures[], reproducible: true }`
- `commonTrap[] { misreading, why_wrong, correct_reading }` — the misinterpretation, made explicit
- `howToReadInAPaper[]` (the checklist)
- `assumptionsAndLimits[]` (e.g. proportional-hazards assumption for HR; censoring assumptions)
- `claims[] { statement, sourceRef, sourceType: peer-reviewed|textbook|guideline }`
- `provenance { sources[], dataManifestRefs[], generatedFiguresChecksum }`
- `framing` — standing **"educational, not clinical advice"** notice (required, structural)
- `accessibility { altTextComplete: true, readabilityScore, colorblindSafeFigures: true }`
- `review { statsReviewer, statsSignoff: required, clinicalReviewer, plainLanguageReviewer, status: draft|stats-reviewed|published }`
- `specVersions { template, curriculumMap, toolkit, datasets[] }`

**LLM authoring stance (model-neutral, structured, source-grounded).** Drafting uses an LLM behind
a thin provider interface (Elyos donated lane: the human runs their own agent; a future funded lane
runs via `packages/runner` with a hard budget cap). The model **drafts** prose and **proposes**
worked examples, but it is **never the authority on a statistical fact or a number**: every claim
must trace to a recorded authoritative source, every figure must be produced by the committed
reproducibility script (deterministic code, not model output), and the biostatistician sign-off is
the final accuracy authority. No model-emitted statistic ships unverified. (Anthropic Claude is the
first-supported reasoning model behind that interface; the seam is vendor-agnostic.)

**Tech stack.** TypeScript, ESM, pnpm workspaces (Elyos conventions). Explainers authored in
Markdown; the figure/interactive toolkit is small Node/TS packages with minimal dependencies;
statistical computation for worked examples uses well-established open methods and committed,
version-pinned open/synthetic datasets, with results emitted deterministically and checksummed.
Output as a static site + downloadable PDFs + the raw Markdown; figures as SVG/PNG generated in CI.
No runtime service; everything runs locally or in CI.

**Pinned versions** (recorded in `specVersions`, bumped only via a deliberate task): authoring
template version; curriculum-map version; toolkit version; each example dataset's snapshot/version
and license; cited-guideline versions (CONSORT/SAMPL/STROBE/ICH E9 editions). Any drift is an
isolated version-bump task, never a silent content change.

**Key decisions.**
- **Correct-first**: biostatistician sign-off is blocking and a stated falsehood fails the release.
- **Reproducible-figures-only**: code, not the model and not hand-typing, produces every number.
- **Teach the trap**: every explainer explicitly names and corrects the common misreading.
- **Open/synthetic data only**: no individual patient data, ever; each dataset license verified.
- **Methods education, not advice**: a structural "not clinical advice" frame; treatment guidance is
  out of scope and escalates to `high`.
- **Template-first**: one authoring object; site, PDF, and checklist cards are projections.

## Data, licensing & compliance

**This is the critical section and it leads with the cancer guardrails.** Although the output is
*educational prose about statistics* (not a patient dataset), we treat licensing and privacy
conservatively because every worked example uses *some* data and every claim quotes others' work.

**Hard data boundary (binding).**
- **In bounds:** **synthetic/simulated** survival data we generate; **openly-licensed teaching
  datasets** that are de-identified by construction and widely used for survival-analysis teaching
  (license verified per dataset, e.g. the classic open survival datasets distributed with open-
  source statistics packages); **published aggregate statistics** from open-access sources
  (e.g. SEER/GLOBOCAN-style aggregate survival/incidence summaries, or aggregate figures from
  CC-BY open-access papers) used only as *aggregate* illustration; **pseudo-data reconstructed from
  an openly-licensed published Kaplan–Meier figure** (synthetic-by-construction, labelled as such,
  only from CC-BY/CC0 figures whose license permits derivatives).
- **Out of bounds, no exceptions:** **individual patient data**, **dbGaP/EGA/individual-level
  biobanks**, controlled-access tiers, and **any identifiable patient records** — including
  individual-level rows in article supplementary files. These require authorized access + IRB and
  are categorically excluded; this is a *teaching* project and needs none of them.

**Per-dataset license verification (data manifest).** Every example dataset is recorded in a
committed **data manifest** with: source, version/snapshot, license (id + evidence/URL),
de-identification status, and whether redistribution/derivatives are permitted. **Objective PASS
criterion:** a dataset is usable only if its license is recorded **and** permits our teaching use
(and redistribution where we redistribute it); missing/unparseable license = **EXCLUDE**, never
default-allow. Synthetic data carries its generating script and is unambiguously redistributable.

**Source/citation licensing (claims ledger).** Conceptual claims cite authoritative methods sources
(peer-reviewed biostatistics literature, recognized textbooks, reporting guidelines). We **cite and
paraphrase facts**; we do **not** copy copyrighted figures, tables, or extended passages. Reporting
guidelines (CONSORT, SAMPL, STROBE, PRISMA, ICH E9) and most are freely citable; where a textbook
figure would be ideal, we **recreate** an equivalent figure from open/synthetic data rather than
reproduce the copyrighted original.

**Output license.** Explainer content and figures are released **CC-BY-4.0** (attribution to
sources preserved); the figure/interactive **toolkit code is MIT**; example **datasets** we
generate or redistribute carry their own recorded open license (synthetic → CC0/CC-BY; redistributed
open datasets keep their upstream license, recorded in the manifest). A release ships **only** with
data whose license permits redistribution; anything consult-only is cited, not bundled.

**Provenance model (every assertion, no exceptions).** Each explainer records: for every conceptual
claim, a resolvable citation to an authoritative source (with source type); for every quantitative
claim, the dataset ref + the committed script + the regenerated figure's checksum. An explainer with
an uncited statistical claim or an unreproducible number is invalid and cannot ship.

**Individual-data / PII stance.** This project needs **no** patient-level data and stores none. The
only realistic leakage paths are (1) accidentally using a non-open or individual-level dataset for an
example, and (2) reconstructing identifiable pseudo-data from a published curve. Both are gated: the
data manifest excludes anything non-open or individual-level, and curve-derived pseudo-data is
synthetic-by-construction from openly-licensed figures only and never claims to be real patients. We
never re-identify and never host identifiable records.

**Attribution & framing.** Every explainer attributes its sources, and carries a standing,
**structural** "**educational, not clinical advice**" notice: the content teaches how to *read*
statistics and must not be used as treatment guidance, prognosis, or a substitute for biostatistical
consultation on a specific study.

## Quality, review & risk gates

**Risk tier: medium** for the clinician-facing explainers and toolkit (statistical/methodological
accuracy + license judgement required). **Standing escalation rule:** any **patient-facing**
rendering, any treatment/prognosis guidance, or any "which therapy is better" conclusion is **out of
scope here** and, if ever undertaken, escalates to **`riskTier: high`** with **credentialed
oncologist + patient-advocate sign-off** and a "not medical advice" frame before merge.

**Required review before any explainer is "done":**
- **Statistical-accuracy reviewer (mandatory, every explainer, non-skippable):** a credentialed
  biostatistician or epidemiologist verifies every definition, interpretation, "common trap",
  assumption, and computed figure. This is the accuracy gate; a single stated statistical falsehood
  fails the release (zero tolerance). May rotate among ≥ 2 qualified reviewers, but ≥ 1 named
  qualified reviewer must always exist or authoring halts.
- **Clinical-framing reviewer (mandatory, every explainer):** an oncologist/clinician confirms the
  framing is clinically sensible, non-misleading, and stays *methods education* — no treatment
  advice creeps in.
- **Licensing & provenance reviewer (mandatory, every release):** every example dataset license
  recorded and permitted; every conceptual claim cited; every number reproducible; no copyrighted
  figure reproduced.
- **Plain-language / accessibility reviewer:** readability target met, alt-text complete,
  colorblind-safe figures, checklist usable.
- **Technical reviewer:** template validity, reproducibility harness, toolkit tests, CI green.
- **Oncologist + patient-advocate sign-off:** required **only** if/when a patient-facing derivative
  is undertaken (out of scope here → `riskTier: high`).

**Accuracy & reproducibility checks (so quality is measured, not vibes).**
- **Reproducibility gate:** CI regenerates every worked-example figure/number from its committed
  script on its cited open/synthetic dataset and diffs against the published values; any mismatch
  fails the build.
- **Provenance gate:** CI checks every conceptual claim in the claims ledger has a resolvable source
  ref and every example dataset is recorded in the manifest with a permitted license.
- **Zero-falsehood rule:** any released explainer found to contain a statistical falsehood (or a
  wrong "correction") is a release-failing defect, corrected via a versioned erratum.
- **Comprehension check (pilot):** a short pre/post misconception quiz on a pilot reader cohort
  measures whether the explainer actually corrects the target misreading.

**Definition of Shipped.** A **versioned, biostatistician-signed-off explainer** (or release of
explainers), with 100% reproducible worked examples, complete provenance, verified open-data
licensing, accessibility met, and a structural "not clinical advice" frame — **adopted/used by a
named beneficiary** (taught from / embedded in a curriculum, journal-club kit, or advocacy training),
recorded by the Steward as an acceptance evidence artifact (`outcomes/<release-id>.json`). Writing an
explainer is *not* shipped; consumed-by-a-beneficiary is.

## Roadmap & milestones

**M0 — Foundation & cold-start (thin)**
- Goal: lock the template, curriculum map, provenance/data model, and review gates; prove the
  end-to-end flow on 1–2 pilot explainers with biostatistician sign-off; begin beneficiary outreach.
- **Cold-start de-risking.** To avoid producing a library nobody teaches from, M0 ships the two
  highest-yield, most-misread concepts first — **hazard ratio (with proportional-hazards)** and
  **Kaplan–Meier/censoring** — and aligns to a realistic adoption path in priority order: (a) an
  informal channel (a training program / journal-club lead who agreed to pilot the explainers);
  failing that, (b) a self-serve fallback — a published, versioned CC-BY explainer pair + checklist
  cards an educator can adopt directly. Synergy note: `oncology-data-literacy` and the Ewing-track
  family guides are downstream consumers if those programs want clinician-facing companion material.
- Exit criteria: (1) authoring template + curriculum map published and validated; (2) claims-ledger
  + data-manifest model and the licensing/data gate authored and applied to the pilot; (3)
  reproducibility harness running in CI on the pilot's open/synthetic example datasets (figures
  regenerate and diff-match); (4) ≥ 2 pilot explainers (HR, KM) authored, **biostatistician-signed-
  off** and clinical-framing-reviewed, with 100% reproducible figures and full provenance; (5) a
  short pre/post comprehension check piloted on ≥ 1 reader group; (6) ≥ 1 beneficiary-outreach thread
  opened; (7) the statistical-accuracy reviewer + licensing/provenance reviewer roles **named**
  (blocking); (8) either adopted via an informal/self-serve channel (acceptance artifact recorded) or
  published with the adoption blocker surfaced.

**M1 — Core survival-analysis set hardened**
- Goal: complete and harden the foundational survival/trial-reading explainers; report real numbers.
- Exit criteria: (1) the core set authored and signed off — censoring/KM, median survival & at-risk
  tables, HR + proportional hazards, log-rank, Cox regression, CI vs p-value, absolute vs relative &
  NNT/NNH; (2) reproducibility gate = 100% enforced in CI (no unreproducible number ships); (3)
  provenance gate green (every claim cited, every dataset licensed); (4) readability + accessibility
  targets met on the whole set; (5) comprehension lift measured on ≥ 3 core misconceptions; (6) ≥ 1
  confirmed adopting beneficiary **or** a documented self-serve adoption by a verifiable third party.

**M2 — Advanced/bias set, interactive toolkit & release v1.0**
- Goal: add the advanced trial-reading/bias topics and interactive figures; ship a consumable release.
- Exit criteria: (1) advanced explainers authored and signed off — surrogate endpoints vs OS, RMST,
  competing risks, immortal-time & lead-time bias, subgroup analysis & multiplicity, non-inferiority/
  equivalence, forest plots & heterogeneity; (2) interactive toolkit (e.g. KM generator, HR-under-
  non-PH simulator) released MIT, running on open/synthetic data, tested in CI; (3) release **v1.0**
  (versioned, DOI'd, CC-BY content + MIT toolkit) covering the core + advanced set; (4) "how to read
  it in a paper" **checklist deck** (journal-club/tumor-board ready) published; (5) ≥ 6 explainers
  **adopted/in active use** by a named beneficiary (evidence artifacts recorded).

**M3 — Reuse, translation-readiness, corrections & sustainability**
- Goal: demonstrate downstream reuse and a maintenance model.
- Exit criteria: (1) ≥ 3 verifiable external reuse/adoption events; (2) a **corrections/erratum**
  process (anyone can flag a statistical error; corrections are versioned and attributed); (3)
  **translation-readiness** (text/figure separation + a localization-ready export, synergy with
  `oncology-glossary-multilingual`); (4) a **refresh** process tracking updated guideline editions
  and dataset/toolkit drift; (5) a named steward for ongoing adopter liaison and outcome tracking.

Dependencies: M1 depends on M0's template/gates/harness; M2's advanced topics + toolkit depend on
M1's hardened core; M3 depends on adopted releases from M1–M2.

## Security & privacy

- **Threat surface is small** (no runtime clinical service, no patient data hosting). Main surfaces:
  CI, the figure/interactive toolkit, and the published explainers + example datasets.
- **Secrets handling:** open/synthetic datasets and citable sources need no secret credentials. If a
  partner/OER submission ever needs a token, it is supplied by the human submitting and never written
  to logs, receipts, or committed files (per Elyos rules). A future funded-lane run uses an API key
  only via `packages/runner` with a hard budget cap; keys never enter logs/receipts.
- **PII / individual data:** the project needs and stores **no** patient-level data; the only
  leakage paths (non-open example dataset; identifiable curve reconstruction) are closed by the
  licensing/data gate. We never re-identify.
- **Abuse/misuse prevention:** refuse and flag any task steering the output toward treatment advice,
  patient-specific calculation, evidence grading presented as clinical recommendation, or laundering
  a non-open dataset as open. The content stays descriptive, source-grounded, methods-education-only.
- **Refusal guardrails (Elyos):** any task drifting into unqualified high-stakes (clinical) advice,
  privacy violation, or license violation is refused and surfaced, not worked around.

## Sustainability & maintenance

- **Post-delivery ownership:** the steward maintains adopter/educator relationships and records
  adoption/reuse; the maintainer keeps the template, toolkit, and pinned dataset/guideline versions
  current.
- **Refresh:** a documented process tracks updated reporting-guideline editions, dataset/toolkit
  drift, and evolving best-practice (e.g. growing use of RMST); stale content becomes `maintenance`
  tasks; releases are versioned (DOI'd) so adopters can pin.
- **Corrections / errata:** a public process lets anyone flag a statistical error; corrections are
  versioned and attributed; a confirmed falsehood triggers an erratum and a review of related
  explainers.
- **Outcome tracking:** the steward records adoption/reuse events and the comprehension-check
  results against the success metrics each milestone.
- **Translation-readiness:** text/figure separation keeps the door open for `oncology-glossary-
  multilingual` to localize the set without re-authoring.
