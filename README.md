Clinicians read trial results every week and act on them, but the **statistics that decide how a result should change practice are routinely misread** — by trainees and senior physicians alike. The literature documents persistent, specific errors: interpreting a **hazard ratio** as if it were a simple risk ratio or a constant "X% lower risk over the whole study"; reading a **Kaplan–Meier** curve's tail without regard to how few patients remain at risk; treating a non-significant **p-value** as proof of no effect, or a significant one as proof of importance; conflating **relative** risk reduction with **absolute** benefit (and ignoring number-needed-to- treat); trusting **surrogate endpoints** (PFS, ORR, pCR) as if they were overall survival; and being fooled by **immortal-time bias**, **lead-time bias**, **competing risks**, post-hoc **subgroup** claims, and **non-inferiority** framing. These are not esoteric: they change whether a drug looks worth its toxicity and cost.
